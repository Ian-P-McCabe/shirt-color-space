Dodgeball League Color Problem Part 2:

Finding the Perfect Colors

by Ian McCabe

The aim of part 1 was to figure out which shirt colors are the best to add to those already selected for the season. We wanted to figure out which colors are the farthest from those already chosen by the league. We used Lab-Color-Distance formulas to select colors that are most distant, and chose from those.

We ended up doing part 1 because teams are allowed to select from 103 unique shirt colors, so it is easy for teams to select colors that are too similar. When teams choose colors that are too similar, one team must then select a new color that is deemed from all the other colors, so it's easy to tell teams apart. But what if we wanted to avoid this situation entirely?

Instead of letting teams pick from 103 colors, we gave them 16 or 24 colors to pick from that would guarantee that all the colors are distinct to the human eye.

Lots of Combinations!

One way to approach this problem is to select the 16 colors that have the greatest average distance between them. This would work, however...

If we want to pick 16 colors, we must run 2,245,547,413,628,550,570 calculations! (2 quintillion)

And if we want to pick 24 colors, we must run 178,409,928,551,259,450,861,900 calculations! (178 sextillion)

To put those numbers into context, it would take ~10 days of compute time to find the 16 best colors, and ~ 2175 years, 328 days, and 17 hours to find the 24 best colors!

The Maximum Diversity Problem

What we're attempting to do, finding a subset of colors with an optimal distance between them, is also known as the Maximum Diversity Problem or MDP. The Maximum Diversity Problem falls into a class of computer problems that are NP-Hard, or functionally unsolvable. We see the NP-Hard characteristics of our color selection problem when the compute time goes from 10 days to 2175 years by adding 8 additional colors to the number of colors we are selecting.

We can work around the complexity of our problem with a heuristic. A heuristic is a problem-solving technique that uses shortcuts to find an approximate solution quickly.

Heuristic 1: Randomly Select Points

A simple way to pick colors is to randomly select them from the set of 103, this likely won't yeild the best results, but it will give us a good baseline for future algorithms.

Heuristic 2: Greedy Algorithms

Greedy algorithms work by picking the best option at each step of the algorithm. In our case, the greedy algorithm will take two points and then iteratively select the 14 points that have the greatest minimum distance from the current set.

We can easily run this algorithm on every combination of points, as 103 choose 2 is only 5253.
